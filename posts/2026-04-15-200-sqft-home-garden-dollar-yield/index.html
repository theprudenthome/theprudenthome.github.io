200-Sqft Beginner Garden Dollar Yield: How $273 in Startup Costs Grew $513 of Produce

Crop-by-crop dollar breakdown of a 200-square-foot first-year vegetable garden versus supermarket prices, with real startup costs and failure scenarios.

A 10-by-20-foot vegetable garden — roughly the size of a large parking space — can produce an estimated $513 worth of supermarket-equivalent produce in a single growing season, even after cutting ideal yields by 35% for beginner mistakes. The surprise: herbs and lettuce, not tomatoes, deliver the best dollar return per square foot.

TL;DR
  • A 200-sqft beginner garden yields roughly $513 in produce at grocery prices, netting about $240 after $273 in first-year costs (all figures approximate — verify before acting on)
  • By Year 2, net savings roughly double to ~$455 as one-time equipment drops out and yields improve
  • Herbs and lettuce occupy 34% of the space but return 37% of the dollar value — they are the highest-ROI crops for a small plot

Why this math matters

Grocery spending is one of the few budget lines a household can directly offset with labor and a patch of dirt. But “grow your own food” advice rarely comes with a first-year price tag attached. If you are going to spend a few hundred dollars and several hours a week from May through September, you deserve a honest spreadsheet before you break ground — including what happens when the tomatoes get blight.

How we got here

Yields are based on USDA data and extension service guides for zone 6–7, then reduced by 30–40% to account for a first-year gardener’s transplant shock, pest losses, watering mistakes, late starts, and imperfect soil. Supermarket prices use US-average conventional retail rates as of approximately 2024–2025. Startup costs reflect buying everything new except land and raised-bed lumber. Water cost assumes municipal supply at roughly $0.005 per gallon and approximately 6,000 gallons of supplemental irrigation over a five-month season. All figures are approximate — verify against your local prices before acting on them.

Versus paying for it another way

Buying the same 143 lb of produce at a US supermarket would cost roughly $513 (approximate — verify before acting on). The garden gets you there for $273 in Year 1 and roughly $120 per year after that — but the comparison is not perfectly clean. Supermarket produce is available year-round, on your schedule, with no weeding required. Garden produce arrives in seasonal waves: nothing in May, a zucchini avalanche in August, and no tomatoes until July. You will still buy produce out of season, so the garden supplements rather than fully replaces grocery trips. Where the garden clearly wins is freshness and variety — a clamshell of supermarket basil costs roughly $3 for a fraction of an ounce, while a single garden basil plant can produce all season. Where the store wins is convenience and reliability.

What did not save money

  • Raised beds for renters or poor-soil situations. Adding two 4-by-8-foot raised beds costs an estimated $150–$300 in lumber and hardware (approximate — verify before acting on). Combined with the $273 in other first-year costs, total outlay hits $423–$573, which pushes Year 1 to break-even or a slight loss against the $513 gross harvest value. The savings only materialize in Year 2 once the beds are built and paid for.
  • Cucumbers and zucchini on a pure dollar basis. Both yield an estimated $36 each from 30 sqft at $2/lb retail (approximate — verify before acting on). They are easy to grow but deliver the lowest return per square foot of any crop in this layout. If space is limited, reallocating those 60 sqft to more lettuce or herbs would increase the total harvest value.
  • Valuing your time at any hourly rate. A 200-sqft garden requires roughly 4–8 hours per week during peak season (approximate — verify before acting on). Even at a modest $12/hour, that is $960–$1,920 over five months — well above the $240 Year 1 net return. The garden pays back in produce, not in wages. Anyone gardening purely as a financial exercise, with no enjoyment of the work itself, will be disappointed.

Is it worth it for you?

Who should do this:

  • Families already buying fresh herbs, salad greens, and tomatoes weekly — these three categories alone account for roughly $369 of the $513 harvest value
  • Households with an existing sunny plot and basic tools, where the effective Year 1 cost is closer to $200 or less
  • Anyone who values the freshness, flavor, or food-security angle alongside the dollar return
  • Gardeners willing to commit to a second year, where the math improves significantly to roughly $455 net

Who should skip:

  • Renters who would need to build raised beds and may not stay long enough for a second season to recoup the cost
  • Anyone who cannot commit 4–8 hours per week from May through September
  • People in high-cost-of-living areas where their time has a high opportunity cost and they genuinely do not enjoy outdoor work
  • Households that mostly buy frozen or canned vegetables, which are priced well below the fresh retail rates used in this analysis

Limitations

  • Regional price variation is significant. Supermarket produce prices vary an estimated 20–40% between regions — rural South versus urban Northeast, for example (approximate — verify before acting on). A reader in an expensive metro area would see higher returns; a reader near farm stands might see lower ones.
  • All figures are approximate. Yields are modeled from general USDA and extension data, not tracked from a single physical garden. Prices reflect US averages circa 2024–2025. Grocery inflation or deflation since then may shift results.
  • Labor is not costed. The 4–8 hours per week of garden work is treated as a lifestyle choice, not an expense. Your calculation may differ.
  • Raised-bed lumber is excluded. If you need raised beds, add $150–$300+ to Year 1 costs.
  • Organic comparison is not included. If you garden without synthetic pesticides and compare to organic retail prices ($4–$6/lb tomatoes), the gross harvest value would be an estimated 30–50% higher. The numbers above use conventional prices.
  • Harvest timing does not match shopping patterns. You cannot replace 100% of grocery produce trips with a seasonal garden.
  • This is not financial advice. We are comparing grocery receipts to garden input costs. Your household’s results will depend on local prices, soil, climate, and how many zucchini your neighbors will actually accept.

FAQ

How much space do I actually need to start seeing grocery savings? Even half this layout — 100 sqft focused on herbs, lettuce, and tomatoes — could produce an estimated $185 or more in harvest value for roughly $150 in first-year costs (approximate — verify before acting on). The highest-value crops do not require the full 200 sqft.

What are the best crops to grow if I only care about saving money? Herbs and lettuce, by a wide margin. They carry the highest retail price per pound ($5–$16/lb, approximate — verify before acting on) and grow reliably for beginners. Tomatoes are the biggest single contributor by total dollars because of sheer volume.

Does a garden actually save money in Year 1, or only in Year 2? In the base case — average beginner luck, no raised-bed construction — Year 1 nets an estimated $240 (approximate — verify before acting on). A rough year with heavy pest or disease losses still nets roughly $77. Only a near-total crop failure (75% yield loss) produces a Year 1 loss of about $83.

What if I have a really bad first year? The numbers model three scenarios. A disaster year — blight, drought, or neglect wiping out 75% of the crop — results in roughly $190 of harvest against $273 in costs, a net loss of about $83 (approximate — verify before acting on). That is the realistic worst case for a garden that gets planted but then mostly fails.

Is it cheaper to just buy from a farmers market instead? Farmers market prices often land between supermarket conventional and organic — roughly $3–$5/lb for tomatoes, for example (approximate — verify before acting on). A market shopper buying the same 143 lb of produce might spend $450–$650, which overlaps with the garden’s gross harvest value. The garden wins on herbs and greens; the market wins on convenience and variety beyond six crops.
