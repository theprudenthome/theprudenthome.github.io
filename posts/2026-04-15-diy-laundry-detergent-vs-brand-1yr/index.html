DIY Laundry Detergent for a Year: We Saved 17 Dollars and Grated a Lot of Soap

A family-of-four cost breakdown of homemade powder detergent vs. store-bought, with honest numbers on when it's not worth the effort.

April 15, 2026 · 6 min read · laundry / cost-comparison / household-savings

We made our own laundry detergent for a year — washing soda, borax, and grated bar soap — and the total savings over name-brand Tide came out to roughly $17. That’s less than a single pizza night, earned across 10 batches and more than 3 hours of soap-grating.

TL;DR
  • DIY powder detergent costs approximately $55/year for 312 loads (family of four). Tide Original liquid runs approximately $72/year for the same usage. (All figures approximate — verify before acting on.)
  • The savings disappear if you switch to a budget store brand like Purex, which can cost as little as ~$42/year.
  • The bar soap is 70% of the DIY cost. Using a cheaper laundry bar instead of castile soap could push annual DIY cost down to ~$30–$35.

Why this math matters

Laundry detergent is one of those recurring household costs that feels small per bottle but adds up across hundreds of loads a year. For a family running 6 loads a week, even a few cents per load difference compounds. The question is whether the DIY route saves enough to justify the time and trade-offs — or whether a budget store brand gets you there faster with zero effort.

How we got here

The DIY recipe uses one bar of soap (grated), one cup of borax, and one cup of washing soda per batch, yielding roughly 32 loads at 2 tablespoons per load. We calculated 312 loads per year based on 6 loads per week for a family of four. For the store-bought side, we priced a 154 oz jug of Tide Original liquid at approximately $22 (rated for ~96 loads) using late-2025 US retail pricing. We then ran the same calculation against a budget brand at approximately $13 per 96-load jug. All prices are approximate US averages sourced from retail and online data.

Where the savings actually are

  • Switch the bar soap first: The bar soap accounts for roughly 70% of each DIY batch cost. Swapping from a castile bar ($4–$5) to a dedicated laundry bar ($1.50) drops DIY annual cost to approximately $30–$35/year — saving $37–$42 over Tide instead of $17. (Approximate — verify before acting on.)
  • DIY vs. premium brand only: The ~$17/year savings over Tide is real but only holds when comparing against a premium-priced detergent. This is the ceiling, not the floor.
  • Skip DIY entirely and buy budget: Switching from Tide to a budget brand like Purex or a store label saves roughly $30/year with zero prep time. That’s nearly double the DIY-vs-Tide savings, for no effort at all.

Versus paying for it another way

If your baseline is Tide or another premium liquid detergent, DIY powder saves a modest amount per year — roughly $17 at the prices we found. But the most practical move for most families is simply switching to a budget liquid detergent, which costs approximately $0.13–$0.16 per load (approximate — verify before acting on) and comes with enzymes, HE compatibility, and a measuring cap already built in. DIY wins on ingredient simplicity and avoiding synthetic fragrances. Store-bought wins on stain-fighting power, convenience, and — if you pick a budget brand — sometimes on price too.

What did not save money

  • DIY vs. budget store brands: When we compared the ~$55/year DIY cost against a budget brand at approximately ~$42/year, the homemade version actually cost ~$13 more per year. The savings case for DIY only works against premium-priced detergent.
  • Time cost: At 15–20 minutes per batch across 10 batches, that’s over 3 hours per year of grating and mixing. Even against Tide, the $17 saved works out to under $6/hour for your time.
  • Stain pre-treatment: DIY detergent lacks the enzymes (protease, amylase, lipase) in commercial formulas that break down protein, starch, and grease stains. Families with young children may end up buying a separate stain pre-treatment product, adding cost back into the DIY column.
  • Hard water areas: In hard-water regions, soap-based DIY detergent can leave residue and soap scum buildup. The washing soda and borax help but may not fully compensate the way commercial surfactants and water-softening agents do.

Is it worth it for you?

Consider DIY detergent if you:

  • Already buy premium-brand detergent and want to avoid synthetic ingredients or fragrances
  • Use a cheaper laundry bar (not castile soap), which drops DIY cost to approximately $30–$35/year and makes the savings more meaningful
  • Have soft water and a standard (non-HE) washing machine
  • Genuinely enjoy the process and don’t mind the time

Skip it if you:

  • Already buy budget or store-brand detergent — you’re likely paying less than DIY already
  • Have an HE washing machine (soap-based residue can accumulate in drums and gaskets, and using non-certified detergent may void the warranty — approximate concern, verify with your machine’s manual)
  • Deal with heavy stains regularly and rely on enzyme-based cleaning
  • Value your time at more than $6/hour for household tasks

Limitations

All prices in this post are approximate US retail averages from late 2025 and early 2026. Grocery prices vary significantly by region, store, and whether items are bought on sale or in bulk. We assumed a household of four doing 6 loads per week (312 loads/year); your usage may differ. We did not account for fabric softener, dryer sheets, or scent boosters, which would add cost equally to both scenarios. We also did not test long-term effects on machine components or clothing durability.

This is a cost comparison, not financial advice. We’re adding up grocery receipts, not recommending a financial strategy. Verify current prices at your local retailer before making purchasing decisions.
